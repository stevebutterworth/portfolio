Why I run SQLite in production, and you might too

Everyone assumes you need Postgres and a fleet of servers. For a surprising number of real apps, you do not. Here is what one file, Solid Queue and Kamal replaced.
Steve Butterworth
12 Jun 2026 · 8 min read · Rails, SQLite

For years I reached for Postgres by reflex. It is a fine default. But defaults are worth questioning, and on the last three projects I shipped, SQLite in production has been not just adequate but genuinely better.

One writer, and why that is fine

The usual objection is the single writer. In practice, if your transactions are short and your jobs are thin, one writer goes a very long way. Solid Queue keeps background work off the request path, and the whole thing stays boring.

The best infrastructure is the kind you forget you have.

Here is the entire production config that matters:

# config/database.yml
production:
  adapter: sqlite3
  database: storage/production.sqlite3
  pragmas:
    journal_mode: wal
    synchronous: normal

What actually broke

Two things, and both were my fault: a long-running write transaction I should have batched, and a backup script that copied the file mid-write. Fixing the first and snapshotting the volume instead solved both.

  • Keep write transactions short and delegate to jobs.
  • Never copy the live .sqlite3 or its WAL; snapshot the volume.
  • Let Kamal handle deploys and migrations on the box.

Would I run it for a bank? No. For most products, though, the honest answer is that it is faster to build, cheaper to run, and easier to hand over.
